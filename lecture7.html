Improper Integrals

How do we evaluate integrals where one bound goes off to infinity?

Definitions:

  1. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_a^{\infty} f(x)\, dx = \lim\limits_{b \rightarrow \infty}\displaystyle \int\limits_a^b f(x) dx\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_{-\infty}^{{b}} f(x)\, dx = \lim\limits_{a \rightarrow -\infty}\displaystyle \int\limits_a^b f(x)\, dx\)

If the limit exists, we say the integral converges. If not, we say it diverges.

What do you notice about this region?
Question: If \(\lim\limits_{x \rightarrow \infty} f(x) \neq 0\), then can \(\displaystyle \int\limits_0^{\infty} f(x)dx\) converge? Why or why not?
Example: Compute the following improper integral: \(\displaystyle \int_0^{\infty} \frac{{1}}{{x}} dx\). Since \(\frac{{1}}{{x}} \rightarrow 0\), must this integral converge?

Limits at infinity / Divergence

  • If the limit of a function at infinity is not \(0\), then the improper integral \(must\) diverge.
  • If the limit is \(0\), then we do not know about the improper integral! The integral can diverge even if the limit at infinity is \(0\).

Discontinuities

\(\displaystyle \int_0^4 \frac{{1}}{\sqrt{4-x}}\,dx\) - any ideas?

Solution: setup a limit \[\lim\limits_{b \rightarrow 4^-} \int\limits_0^b \frac{{1}}{\sqrt{4-x}}dx\] Re-write the integrand as \((4-x)^{-\frac{{1}}{{2}}}\), and use the power rule: \[\lim\limits_{b \rightarrow 4^-}\displaystyle \int\limits_0^b(4-x)^{-1/2}\,dx =\lim\limits_{b\rightarrow 4^-}-2(4-x)^{1/2}\Big|_0^b\]

Continuing:\(\lim\limits_{b\rightarrow 4^-}\big( -2(4-b)^{1/2} + 2(4)^{1/2}\big) = \lim\limits_{b\rightarrow 4^-} \big(-2(4-b)^{1/2} + 4\big)\) As \(b \rightarrow 4^{-}\), what happens to \((4-b)^{1/2}\)? It approaches 0! So the limit is just 4!
Question: why is it important here that we approached 4 from the left?

Middle of Interval

\(\displaystyle\int_{-1}^1 \frac{{1}}{x^2}\,dx\) - any ideas?

Idea: Split into 2 integrals. \[\displaystyle\int_{-1}^1 \frac{{1}}{x^2}\,dx= \begin{align} &\lim_{b\rightarrow 0^-} \int_{-1}^b \frac{{1}}{x^2} dx &+ \lim_{a \rightarrow 0^+} \int \frac{{1}}{x^2} dx \\ &= \lim_{b\rightarrow 0^-} \left. (-\frac{{1}}{{x}}) \right|_{-1}^b &+ \lim_{a \rightarrow 0^+} \left.(-\frac{{1}}{{x}})\right|_a^1 \\ &= \ \lim_{b \rightarrow 0^-} 1 - \frac{{1}}{{b}} &+ \lim_{a \rightarrow 0^+} -1 + \frac{{1}}{{a}} \end{align}\] Both integrals diverge.

Areas between curves (preview)

Determine the area of the region bounded by the curves \(y=x\) and \(y=x^2\).

Ideas? First: Where do these curves intersect? What are the endpoints of the region?

\[\begin{align} x &= x^2 \\ x - x^2 &= 0 \\ x(1 - x) &= 0 \\ x = 0 \text{ or } x &= 1 \end{align}\]

Whatâ€™s the area under just one of those curves?

\[\int_0^1 x dx = \left.\frac{x^2}{2}\right|_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}\] \[\int_0^1 x^2 dx = \left.\frac{x^3}{3}\right|_0^1 = \frac{1}{3}\]

Then just subtract those areas! \(A = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}\).

Or: take the integral of the difference!

\[\int_0^1 (x - x^2) dx = \left.\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}\right|_0^1 - \frac{1}{6}\]

Question: Why \(x - x^2\) and not \(x^2 - x\)?

Strategy

  1. Determine where they intersect
  2. Determine which function is greater in this region
  3. Set up the integral \(\int_a^b |f(x) - g(x)| dx\).

(Use (2) to determine whether the integral is of \(f(x) - g(x)\) or \(g(x) - f(x)\).)
