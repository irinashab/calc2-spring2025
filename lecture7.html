Improper Integrals

How do we evaluate integrals where one bound goes off to infinity?

Definitions:

  1. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_a^{\infty} f(x)\, dx = \lim\limits_{b \rightarrow \infty}\displaystyle \int\limits_a^b f(x) dx\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_{-\infty}^{{b}} f(x)\, dx = \lim\limits_{a \rightarrow -\infty}\displaystyle \int\limits_a^b f(x)\, dx\)

If the limit exists, we say the integral converges. If not, we say it diverges.

Exercise:

  1. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_0^{\infty} xe^{-x^2}\,dx\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int\limits_0^{\infty} e^{-x} \,dx\)

Divergence

How would we find if the integral \(\displaystyle \int_0^{\infty} (e^{-x} + 1)\, dx\) converges or diverges without computing?

What do you notice about this region?
Question: If \(\lim\limits_{x \rightarrow \infty} f(x) \neq 0\), then can \(\displaystyle \int\limits_0^{\infty} f(x)dx\) converge? Why or why not?
Example: Compute the following improper integral: \(\displaystyle \int_0^{\infty} \frac{{1}}{{x}} dx\). Since \(\frac{{1}}{{x}} \rightarrow 0\), must this integral converge?

Limits at infinity / Divergence

  • If the limit of a function at infinity is not \(0\), then the improper integral \(must\) diverge.
  • If the limit is \(0\), then we do not know about the improper integral! The integral can diverge even if the limit at infinity is \(0\).

  1. \(\int_1^{\infty} \frac{{1}}{x^2} dx\)
  2. Does \(\int\limits_0^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx\) converge or diverge?
Hint on (2): compare \(e^{-x^2}\) with \(e^{-x}\), integral of which we already know converges.

Discontinuities

\(\displaystyle \int_0^4 \frac{{1}}{\sqrt{4-x}}\,dx\) - any ideas?

Solution: setup a limit \[\lim\limits_{b \rightarrow 4^-} \int\limits_0^b \frac{{1}}{\sqrt{4-x}}dx\] Re-write the integrand as \((4-x)^{-\frac{{1}}{{2}}}\), and use the power rule: \[\lim\limits_{b \rightarrow 4^-}\displaystyle \int\limits_0^b(4-x)^{-1/2}\,dx =\lim\limits_{b\rightarrow 4^-}-2(4-x)^{1/2}\Big|_0^b\]
