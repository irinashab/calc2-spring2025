Differential Equations - What are they?

A differential equation (abbreviated DE) is a challenge. In a so-called ordinary differential equation (abbreviated ODE), the challenger is thinking of a function, \(y = f(x)\) and gives us a relationship between an unknown function and its derivatives. To solve the equation means to find the function \(f(x)\). A few examples will help.

  • Guess the solution of the differential equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\mathrm{e}^{x}\).

  • Now try \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\mathrm{e}^{2x}\).

  • What about \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x^2\)?

Why do we need differential equations?

  • Scientific experiments: can be easier to observe changes to data

  • Might not know the actual formula describing your data

  • How a quantity changes may be related to the quantity itself (e. g.the more money you have, the more it earns in interest / investment)

Sometimes it can be easier to find the relationship between the change and the quantity. If so, set up a differential equation.

Example

  • Suppose we are growing a bacteria culture.

  • The larger the culture, the bigger the growth of the culture at any given time.

  • Let \(m(t)\) be the mass of the culture at time \(t\) (Can easily measure the mass.)

  • We notice: at any given \(t\) (in seconds?), the culture grows at a rate \(.05m(t)\)

  • Idea: take lots of measurements, compare mass to previous mass. Look for a pattern.

  • Initial mass: \(2\) grams. Mass in \(10\) minutes?

We have a differential equation:\[m^\prime(t) = .05 m(t)\] What functions are proportional to their own derivatives? Exponential functions! Guess \(m(t) = Ae^{kt}\), and use \(m^\prime(t)\) and \(m(0)\) to find these constants \(A\) and \(k\). Solution \[\begin{aligned} m(t) &= Ae^{kt} \\ m^\prime(t) &= Ake^{kt} \\ &= .05 m(t) \\ &= .05 Ae^{kt} \\ k &= .05\end{aligned}\] So we have:\(m(t) = Ae^{.05t}\), \(m(0)=2\). Can we find \(A\)? Indeed, \(Ae^0=2\), therefore \(A=2\). So the answer is \(m(t) = 2e^{.05t}\), \(m(10)=2e^{0.5}\)

Overview

  • Any equation involving the derivative of a quantity is known as a differential equation.

  • Example \(y^\prime = x\)

  • A function \(y=f(x)\) satisfying that equation is called a solution to the differential equation.

  • In general: differential equations do not have unique solutions

  • \(y = \frac{x^2}{2}\) is a solution to \(y^\prime=x\)

  • \(y = \frac{x^2}{2}+1\) is another solution to \(y^\prime=x\)

Order

The order of a differential equation is the highest derivative that appears in the equation.
Exercise: Identify the orders of the following:

  1. \(y^\prime = y + x\)

  2. \((y^\prime)^2 = (y - x)^2\)

  3. \(y^{\prime\prime} = -y\)

  4. \(y^{\prime\prime\prime} + (y^\prime)^5 = y\)

Example

A ball is thrown straight into the air with an initial velocity of 5 meters per second. It is acted on by a constant, downward force of gravity, causing an acceleration of \(-9.8\) \(m/s^2\)

  1. Find a formula \(v(t)\) for the velocity at time \(t\) seconds after the ball is thrown.

  2. How many seconds after the ball is thrown does it reach its maximum height?

  3. If the ball is thrown at a height of 2 meters above the ground, what is the maximum height reached?

Solution

Since \(v^\prime(t) = a(t)\) , and \(a(t)=9.8\), we know:

  • \(v^\prime(t) = -9.8\)

  • \(v^\prime(t) = -9.8\)

So, \(v(t) = -9.8t + C\). Since \(v(0)=5\), we can compute \(5 = -9.8(0) + C\), so \(C=5\). Our formula for the velocity is \(v(t) = -9.8t + 5\) Going back to Calc I:

  • the ball reaches its maximum height when its upward velocity is 0

  • \(v(t) = -9.8t + 5 = 0\)

  • Solve for \(t\), \(t = \frac{-5}{-9.8} \approx .51\) seconds

  • Derivative of height: velocity.

  • \(h^\prime(t) = -9.8t + 5\)

  • So, \(h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 5t + C\)

  • \(h(0)=2\) means \(-4.9(0) + 5(0) + C = 2\)

  • So \(C=2\).

Therefore \(h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 5t + 2\). Then \(h(\frac{5}{9.8}) \approx 3.28\) meters.

Separable Equations

A differential equation of the form \(y^\prime=f(x)g(y)\) is called separable equation.We solve it by separating varaibles: \[\frac{y^\prime}{g(y)} = f(x)\] Then we integrate both sides: the left side with respect to \(y\), and the right side with respect to \(x\): \[\displaystyle\int \frac{dy}{g(y)} = \int f(x) dx\] Let’s do a simple example: \(y^\prime=y\), \(y(0)=1\) We separate variables and set up the integrals:\[\displaystyle\int \frac{dy}{t} = \int dx\] We end up with:\[\ln(y)=x+C\] Solve this for \(y\). Since the inverse of \(\ln(y)=x\) is \(y=e^x\), we have \[y=e^{x+C}\] Since \(e^{x+C}=e^x \cdot e^C\) and \(e^C\) is just another constant, we re-write our solution as \(Ae^x\), where \(A\) is a constant. Now we plug in our initial conditions: \(y(0)=1\) means \(Ae^0=1\) or \(A=1\). Our solution is \(y=e^x\).

As a side note: there are many equivalent ways of defining the number \(e\). One way is the classic limit definition using compound interest: \(e = \lim\limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \Big(1 + \frac{1}{n}\Big)^n\).Another way is to define \(e\) using differential equations: here we used our knowledge of \(e\) and natural logarithms (log base \(e\)) to solve the above differential equation, but you can start with the differential equation \(f^\prime(x) = f(x)\) and \(f(0)=1\), and define \(e\) to be \(f(1)\).

Each of these definitions can be used as a starting point, and then we can prove, mathematically, that the other properties of hold. That is, for example, we could start with using the differential equations definition, and then we can prove that \(f(1)\) is exactly equal to that limit definition.

There are still other definitions of \(e\), your textbook provides one in section 2.7.Why do mathematicians define \(e\) in so many ways? Really because mathematicians tried, for a long time, to make mathematics rigorous. They wanted to find the exact axioms and definitions that we can start with, so that, making those assumptions, we can prove everything else there is to know about mathematics. Calculus was especially tricky to make rigorous; the history of mathematics often involves terms (like limits and continuity) being redefined to try to make the intuitive results actually provable.

Example and check

This is another example of a separable differential equation: \[\begin{aligned} y^\prime &= \frac{x}{y} \\ y(0) &= 2\end{aligned}\] We solve this by separating variables and integrating:\[\int y dy = \int x dx\] Integrating:\[\frac{y^2}{2} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C\] Or \(y^2 = x^2 + C\). Taking square roots,we get either \(y = \sqrt{x^2 + C}\) or \(-y = \sqrt{x^2 + C}\). Since \(y(0) = 2 > 0\), we know that the second option is not possible, so it must be that \(y = \sqrt{x^2 + C}\). Now we plug in our initial condition: \(y(0)=2\) means that \(\sqrt{C} = 2\). herefore \(C=4\), and so the solution to the original equation is \(y = \sqrt{x^2 + 4}\).
We can check that this solution works by plugging it back into the original differential equation (sanity check!). First, it’s clear that this solution satisfies the initial condition, \(y(0)=2\). Next, we show that it satisfies \(y^\prime = \frac{x}{y}\).
Since \(y = (x^2 + 4)^{\frac{1}{2}}\), we take the derivative. Using the power rule and chain rule:
\(y^\prime = \frac{1}{2} (x^2 + 4)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot (2x)\), or \(y^\prime = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+4}}\). But this is exactly \(\frac{x}{y}\), which is what we wanted to show!

Mass Example Again

Problem: A culture of bacteria grows at a rate proportional to its mass. If the culture initially weighs 10g, and in one hour, it weighs 15g, find a formula \(m(t)\) for its mass (in grams) \(t\) hours after that initial measurement.
First, we set up a differential equation: \(m^\prime(t) = km(t)\). We don’t know what \(k\) is, but we know it must be a constant, since the problem states that \(m^\prime\) is proportional to \(m\). Then we have two initial conditions: \(m(0) = 10\) and \(m(1) = 15\). We solve the equation by separating variables: \[\begin{aligned} \int \frac{dm}{m} = \int k dt \\ \ln(m) = kt + C \\ m = e^{kt + C} = e^{kt} e^C\end{aligned}\] Since \(e^C\) is a constant, we can refer to this as another constant \(A\), and our solution is \(m(t) = Ae^{kt}\). Notice that we have two unknown constants here: \(k\) and \(A\). We can use the two initial conditions to find it: \(m(0) = 10\) means that \(Ae^{0} = 10\). Therefore \(A = 10\), so \(m(t) = 10 e^{kt}\). Also, \(m(1) = 15\) means \(10e^{k} = 15\) or \(e^k = 1.5\). Therefore \(k = \ln(1.5) \approx 0.41\). The solution, then, is \(y = 10 e^{\ln(1.5) t}\). Using rules for exponents, \(e^{\ln(1.5) t} = (e^{\ln(1.5)})^t\), and since \(e^{\ln(x)} = x\), we can further simplify our solution to \(m(t) = 10 \cdot 1.5^t\).
